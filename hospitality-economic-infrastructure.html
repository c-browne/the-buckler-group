Hospitality as Economic Infrastructure

Hotels, resorts, residences, and mixed-use assets as engines of destination value.

Christopher A. Browne Founder & CEO, The Buckler Group
Architect of Order | Caribbean Renaissance™ Framework
PublishedJune 2026
IssueNo. 002
Read8 minutes
In this article
Beyond Tourism Destination Infrastructure Hotels as Anchors Mixed-Use Hospitality Hospitality & Entitlement Climate Resilience Regional Competitiveness

Hospitality has traditionally been measured by occupancy rates, visitor arrivals, average daily rates, and tourism receipts. While those metrics remain important, they no longer capture the strategic role hospitality plays in shaping modern economies.

Today, hospitality is infrastructure.

Hotels, branded residences, mixed-use districts, convention centers, marinas, entertainment venues, airports, cruise terminals, and public spaces collectively influence investment patterns, labor markets, infrastructure investment, urban development, and national competitiveness.

For the Caribbean, this shift carries profound implications. The question is no longer how many visitors arrive each year. The more important question is what long-term economic systems those visitors support.

The Caribbean Renaissance™ Framework proposes a different way of thinking about hospitality—not as an industry that serves tourism, but as an economic platform that organizes investment, development, and opportunity across the region.

Beyond Tourism

Hospitality has long been categorized as part of the visitor economy. That classification understates its economic significance.

When thoughtfully planned, hospitality influences nearly every sector of national development. A successful destination requires transportation infrastructure, utilities, digital connectivity, healthcare capacity, environmental management, workforce development, housing, public safety, and cultural institutions.

In this context, a hotel is not merely a building. It is an anchor institution.

Every Great Destination Is Built on Infrastructure

The world’s most competitive hospitality markets did not emerge by accident. They were intentionally built through coordinated investment in transportation, public space, planning, culture, education, and governance.

Visitors may remember a resort. Investors remember the operating environment. Residents experience the supporting infrastructure every day.

The distinction matters because hospitality succeeds most consistently where destinations function efficiently beyond the visitor experience: roads, utilities, airports, public waterfronts, broadband, security, public policy, and land-use planning.

These systems determine whether hospitality assets appreciate in value over decades or struggle to remain competitive.

Hospitality is not merely a sector that serves visitors. It is an enabling system through which transportation, housing, culture, education, infrastructure, capital, and community development intersect.

Hotels as Economic Anchors

Every major hospitality investment produces economic activity beyond its property boundaries. Construction creates employment. Operations support local suppliers. Restaurants stimulate culinary entrepreneurship. Conference facilities attract business travel. Hotels strengthen airline demand. Retail districts expand. Professional services deepen. Universities establish partnerships. Property values increase.

The economic multiplier extends far beyond guest rooms. Hospitality assets become anchors around which entire districts evolve.

This explains why many of the world’s most successful mixed-use developments begin with a hospitality component rather than ending with one. Hospitality creates confidence. Confidence attracts capital. Capital accelerates development.

The Rise of Mixed-Use Hospitality

Increasingly, investors no longer separate hospitality from residential, retail, office, entertainment, and cultural uses. The future belongs to integrated districts.

Branded residences complement luxury hotels. Marinas support waterfront retail. Convention centers stimulate office demand. Public plazas strengthen surrounding real estate. Universities generate executive education programs. Healthcare facilities support wellness tourism.

Rather than isolated assets, successful destinations increasingly function as interconnected ecosystems. For Caribbean jurisdictions, this evolution creates opportunities to move beyond resort development toward complete destination-building.

Hospitality and Entitlement

No hospitality investment begins with architecture. It begins with entitlement.

Before financing is secured or construction begins, developers must navigate zoning, land assembly, environmental approvals, infrastructure capacity, utility coordination, transportation planning, and regulatory compliance. These processes directly influence investment risk.

Institutional capital evaluates entitlement systems with the same scrutiny applied to financial projections. Predictability matters. Transparency matters. Execution matters.

Jurisdictions capable of reducing unnecessary regulatory friction while maintaining rigorous environmental and planning standards become substantially more competitive in attracting long-term investment.

Climate Resilience Is Hospitality Strategy

Climate resilience is often discussed as an environmental concern. It is equally an investment concern.

Resilient infrastructure protects destinations. It reduces insurance costs. It preserves operational continuity. It strengthens investor confidence. It protects employment. It safeguards national competitiveness.

Hospitality developments designed for resilience increasingly command greater institutional interest because they reduce long-term operational uncertainty. For island economies, resilience is not a sustainability initiative. It is an economic necessity.

Lessons from the Caribbean

Throughout the Caribbean Renaissance™ NYC Consular Listening Tour, conversations with Caribbean diplomatic leaders consistently returned to hospitality as a catalyst for broader economic development.

Although every jurisdiction possesses unique priorities, several common themes emerged. Hospitality remains one of the region’s strongest competitive advantages. Yet governments increasingly seek investment that creates enduring local value rather than isolated tourism assets.

There is growing interest in developments that strengthen local supply chains, expand workforce opportunities, encourage entrepreneurship, and stimulate mixed-use investment beyond resort boundaries.

Equally important, discussions reinforced that successful destinations depend upon institutional coordination. Investment promotion agencies, planning authorities, tourism organizations, universities, infrastructure providers, and the private sector all influence the quality of the hospitality investment environment.

Hospitality therefore becomes a test of institutional effectiveness. The strongest destinations are rarely those with the most natural beauty. They are those with the strongest institutions.

Hospitality as a Platform for Regional Competitiveness

The Caribbean competes with destinations across the Mediterranean, Southeast Asia, the Gulf, Central America, and Africa. Natural beauty alone is no longer sufficient.

Future competitiveness will increasingly depend upon destinations capable of integrating hospitality with infrastructure, education, technology, climate resilience, public policy, and investment strategy.

Hospitality should therefore be viewed as an organizing platform for national development. When coordinated effectively, it aligns ministries, investors, universities, utilities, developers, airlines, cultural institutions, and local businesses around shared economic objectives.

That coordination creates stronger destinations. Stronger destinations attract higher-quality investment. Higher-quality investment creates broader prosperity.

Strategic proposition

Hospitality is not the outcome of development. It is one of its most powerful engines. Hotels, resorts, residences, and mixed-use assets become engines of destination value when they are connected to infrastructure, entitlement clarity, local enterprise, and institutional capacity.

From Projects to Places

The next generation of Caribbean hospitality will not be defined by individual resorts. It will be defined by complete places.

Places where visitors choose to stay. Entrepreneurs choose to build. Families choose to live. Students choose to study. Investors choose to deploy capital. Communities choose to remain.

This requires moving beyond transactional development toward long-term place-making. The Caribbean possesses every ingredient necessary to achieve that transition. What remains is the discipline to organize those assets into coherent development systems.

Conclusion

Hospitality should no longer be viewed as a sector of the economy. It is an enabling system through which transportation, housing, culture, education, infrastructure, capital, and community development intersect.

For the Caribbean, this perspective represents more than a conceptual shift. It offers a strategic framework for building stronger destinations, attracting more sophisticated investment, and creating broader economic opportunity for future generations.

The Caribbean Renaissance™ Framework advances a simple proposition: hospitality is not the outcome of development. It is one of its most powerful engines.
